# Reply to your review of v2 — TomeRoam durable-progress plan

Both required edits are made. v3 is written. This reply is short because we mostly
agree; it covers one confirmation you'd want, three things I'd add, and one open
question back to you.

---

## Your two required edits — done

**1. `viewCount` narrowed to completion evidence.** You're right and v2 was sloppy: I
wrote that completion "tells you which chapter you're past", which conflates *a chapter
was finished at some point* with *this is where you are*. It says nothing about
revisits, restarts, or a next chapter entered for under a minute. It is now a **weak
rank-4 fallback** — contiguous completed prefix only, reached solely when ranks 2 and 3
are empty — and it never enters the same candidate pool as `{track, offset, timestamp}`
records. Your five-level hierarchy is adopted as written.

**2. Split authority protocol specified.** Adopted essentially verbatim: parent stays
authoritative → compute both children → write child 0, verify by decoded read-back →
write child 1, verify → replace the **parent's payload** with the versioned redirect →
verify → children authoritative. Your framing is the part that made it click: *every
partial state must have exactly one unambiguous authoritative copy*, and the parent's
own payload is the commit point because a single summary write either lands or doesn't.
Crash safety then falls out of the ordering rather than being bolted on. Reader rules,
the degraded-sync state for a corrupt child, and queuing a mid-split write for the
correct child are all in.

---

## A confirmation you'd want: your timestamp rule is sound, and here's why

You proposed `native.lastViewedAt > floor(board.ts / 1000)` without knowing whether the
two clocks were even comparable. **They are, and it's load-bearing:** the board stamps
records with `Plex.serverNow()` — the *server's* clock, not the device's — and
`lastViewedAt` is server time as well. Same clock, two precisions. So truncating the
board's milliseconds is a valid comparison rather than a hopeful one, and your
same-second tie-break to the board is right on both counts (better time precision, and
usually better position precision given the ~60s floor).

Had the board used device time, your rule would have been comparing clocks that drift
against each other and the whole arbitration would need rethinking. Worth stating
explicitly in case it ever changes.

---

## Your other points

**Two local stores (`authored` / `replica`).** Adopted. This resolves what v2 called
its only decision-blocking question. The properties you list are the reason it works —
immutable records carrying `originDeviceId`, republishable without transferring
authorship, so a later local play produces a genuinely new authored record with a newer
timestamp and wins on merge. No recovery-device election, idempotent replication,
duplicates merging harmlessly. Tombstones get identical treatment and are retained
indefinitely.

**Injectable budget.** Adopted, and it's the most valuable idea in this round. v2's
worst risk — a split path firing once every few years, broken by the time anyone reaches
it — is not mitigated by more tests around a production-only branch; it's *dissolved* by
making the budget a parameter. At 350 bytes ordinary fixtures split recursively on every
run, and the same production code runs at 8–12KB in the field. The rarest path becomes
the most-exercised one, with no special mock to drift out of sync with reality. Your
full partial-state list and the property-based tests against a reference model are both
in.

**Dual-write lifetime.** Adopted as settled: indefinite is the default, no safe
automatic retirement exists (offline-for-years is indistinguishable from gone), and the
alternatives are recorded as explicit compromises rather than dressed up as safe.

**Relay ceiling.** Adopted as downgraded — conservative sizing plus adaptive reduction
plus read-back verification make the exact number operationally unimportant.

**Shared-user isolation.** Adopted as scoped: it gates the cross-app feature only, and
until it's verified with two accounts against one library, native offsets must not
override board records for shared-library users.

## Three things I'd add

**1. An edge in the completion-prefix fallback.** A fully re-listened book has *every*
chapter viewed, so the contiguous prefix yields no unviewed chapter and resolves to
"finished" — which may be right (you did finish it) or wrong (you restarted it and
stopped under the 60s floor). I've accepted it as tolerable because rank 4 is only
reached when ranks 2 and 3 are both empty, and a genuine restart would have produced an
offset or a board record. Flagging in case you disagree.

**2. Replication multiplies storage across boards.** If several devices each republish
the same recovered records, those records exist on several boards. Correct under
last-write-wins, and sharding absorbs the size — but it is real multiplication, and it's
the price of keeping single-writer boards. Recorded as a consequence rather than a
surprise. (No contention risk: each device splits only its own shards, so two devices
never split the same one.)

**3. Permanent redirects build a tree.** Keeping every redirect forever means a binary
tree of playlists — roughly 2N−1 for N leaves. Irrelevant at realistic scale (a shard
holds several hundred books, so most users never reach two), but worth naming. Deleting
a redirect after both children verify is safe **provided routing is longest-prefix-match
over discovered titles**, which is how it's specified — the children are still found
without the parent.
