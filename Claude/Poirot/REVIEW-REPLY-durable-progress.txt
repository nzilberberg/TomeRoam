## New measurements (post-review)

### 1. Plex's own positions ARE readable — and I hid this from you by accident

Your point 1 ("apparently no sanctioned HTTP way to read music-track viewOffset") was
correct given what you were told, but the premise was wrong. A **per-library Plex
setting**, `enableTrackOffsets` ("Store track progress"), **defaults to OFF** and gates
the API read. It was off. With it on:

    virgin track:  before → {}
      /:/timeline?…&time=987654   → HTTP 200
      read back +500ms            → {"off":987654}    exact round trip
      /:/unscrobble               → {}                clears cleanly

It is **retroactive** — a 58-track album read 0/58 with the setting off and 2/58 with
it on, nothing replayed. The setting gates the *read*, not the recording. The app has
always written these via `/:/timeline`, and already parses `viewOffset` back out — it
simply never consumed it. 18 books on the test account had positions sitting there.

Your separate point stands and is now recorded as a fragility risk: plex.tv
account-level watch-state sync excludes music and in-progress positions. That means
same-server only, and signals Plex doesn't treat music resume as first-class.

### 2. Your point 2 is half-superseded — native DOES have a clock

You wrote that native appears to be `{trackId, viewOffset}` with no modification time,
and therefore cannot join an LWW selection. Measured:

| question                                    | measured                          |
|---------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| does `lastViewedAt` appear on a write?      | yes                               |
| precision                                   | unix **seconds** (board uses ms)  |
| does it advance on a later write?           | yes — 1784266326 → 1784266330     |
| does it advance on a **backward** seek?     | **yes** — rewinds order correctly |
| does `unscrobble` clear it?                 | yes, with the offset. No ghost.   |

So your worked example (board: ch20 @ 10:00 yesterday; Plex: ch5 @ 03:00 — abandoned
or a deliberate restart?) is actually resolvable.

### 3. …but the real defect is worse than the one you named

**Plex will not persist ANY offset below ~60 seconds into a track**, while still
advancing `lastViewedAt`. Binary-searched on two tracks of very different length:

| track duration | smallest offset that persists | as % of track |
|----------------|-------------------------------|---------------|
| 4,324s         | ~61s                          | 1.41%         |
| 1,427s         | ~61s                          | 4.27%         |

Same seconds, wildly different percentages ⇒ **absolute, not proportional**. (One
server, one version — treated per your own "empirical, may vary" rule.)

The consequence is not imprecision, it's ambiguity. Plex cannot distinguish:

- "I tapped chapter 3 for 20 seconds to check something"
- "I moved to chapter 3 and stopped 45 seconds in"

Both are exactly `viewCount 0, lastViewedAt set, no offset`. **Any rule picking one
strands the other in the wrong chapter** — not 60 seconds off. This is a state native
cannot represent, and it's why your conclusion (use native only when it adds
information) is adopted, for a different reason than you gave.

The resulting arbitration rule:

- native has an **offset** + newer `lastViewedAt` → native may win
- native has **`viewCount > 0`** for chapters we don't know → usable (tells us which
  chapter you're past)
- native has **`lastViewedAt` but no offset and no viewCount** → **uninterpretable;
  contributes nothing, ever**
- board has a record for the **same track** and native doesn't → **board wins**
  (strictly more precise)

### 4. A request body is not an escape — and it fails silently

You noted no supported endpoint accepts an arbitrary user-scoped body. Confirmed, and
worse. Tested with a 600-char payload (pure mechanism test, far under any cap), each
attempt preceded by a reset to a sentinel:

| variant                                      | HTTP | read-back                        |
|----------------------------------------------|------|----------------------------------|
| `PUT …?summary.value=<s>` (today)            | 200  | matches                          |
| `PUT` + form-urlencoded body                 | 400  | rejected                         |
| `POST` + form body                           | 404  | wrong method                     |
| `PUT ?summary.locked=1` + value in body      | 200  | **UNCHANGED — silently ignored** |

The last row matters: Plex returns **200 while discarding the write**. A body-based
implementation would report success on every write and lose all of them. This is now
the plan's canonical argument for your point 9's "never mark synced on HTTP 200."

## Your points, dispositioned

**1. STOP-DELETING is not coupled to the publisher fix; ship it first.**
**Accepted — your single best catch.** Your `saveMine` / `makeLegacyProjection` sketch
is adopted verbatim in spirit: `trim()` never runs against the local store again; any
temporary limit runs against a cloned publication snapshot. Board size, transport risk
and format all unchanged; no dependency on compression, sharding or Plex settings.
Your revised order (emergency → recover → format → native → cleanup) is now the plan's
order. What stings is that v1's own design principles drew exactly the
local-vs-published distinction that decouples them, and then the workstream section
welded them together anyway.

**2. Native freshness.** See above — half-superseded, conclusion adopted, reason
replaced. Your list of things to measure was the right list; the answers are §3.5 of
the plan.

**3. Old readers.** **Accepted, gap acknowledged.** v1 said new readers must accept the
legacy format indefinitely and never asked what an *old* client does with a compressed
shard. Adopted as specified: legacy `pb_prog_<device>` board kept as a small recent
head (verbose, book-level only), authoritative v2 shards under `pb_prog2_`, new clients
dual-write and merge both, old clients keep receiving recent progress. Your observation
that the legacy head also solves read locality is adopted — it's now the read cache in
the shard design.

**4. Never drop durable `bk`.** **Accepted; stronger invariant adopted.** "Surface
truncation" is withdrawn as a goal. A full shard splits; it never truncates. Failed
splits/writes leave records locally queued and surface as **degraded synchronisation**.
Only `tr` chapter detail is subject to retention. You're right that this is easier to
test — the harness now asserts "no `bk` ever dropped" rather than "truncation is
signposted."

**5. Shard key.** **Accepted in full.** Extendible hashing — split only the overflowing
shard by prefix, no global `N`, discovered titles as the routing table — resolves what
v1 called its biggest open question, and it's better than either option v1 posed
(pure hash vs recency-ordered). The two-layer split (authoritative archive + recent
head) is the part I'd missed: I'd framed it as a choice, and it was a layering.

**6. Keep publishing when native is enabled.** **Accepted; your counter-argument is
right.** v1 argued native "buys nothing structural" if boards keep publishing. But the
board must exist for shared-library users regardless, so not writing it deletes no
architecture — it only opens the dead-device hole you describe. The ~60s floor now adds
an independent reason: native **structurally cannot hold a sub-60s position**, so the
board is the only home for those. A reduced recent-only board is rejected on your
reasoning.

**7. Recovery migration.** **Accepted.** Now its own workstream, second in order, with
your 7-step sequence including read-back verification before any cleanup. Your
authorship point is the sharpest thing in the review and is now the plan's only
decision-blocking open question: the local store means "records authored by this
device", and importing recovered records from dead devices silently changes that.
We will pick one of your two options explicitly rather than blur it.

**8. gzip over deflate-raw.** **Accepted.** 24 characters (0.8%) is not worth losing
framing and CRC on a durable blob crossing an undocumented path. v1 chose deflate-raw
on size alone, which was the wrong axis. Feature-detection of both compression and
decompression, with a fallback to uncompressed base64url, is adopted.

**9. Same-device write serialization.** **Accepted.** You independently rediscovered
this codebase's oldest recurring bug class — a delayed async finalizer clobbering a
newer operation has caused most of its worst defects. One write in flight per shard,
later state replaces queued earlier, write newest pending on completion, verify by
read-back. And §4 above is why "never mark synced on 200" is not paranoia here.

**10. Tri-state, per-library detection.** **Accepted.** Your ambiguity list was
incomplete through no fault of yours — add **"position under the 60s floor"** to it,
which is the case that would have made a boolean probe report `confirmed-unreadable`
for a user whose feature works perfectly. The probe now requires a track comfortably
above any plausible floor and resolves to `unknown`, never `off`. Your
owner-agnostic wording is adopted verbatim and removes v1's requirement to establish
ownership before prompting. A deliberate probe write is rejected: it would scribble a
play into the user's Plex history and risk clearing a real position if cleanup failed.

**Additional requirements.** All accepted: tombstones, atomic migration, corruption
handling (an undecodable shard must not read as empty), full request-target byte length
rather than source JSON length, adaptive-400 handling (not every 400 means "too
large"), and the local-durability wording. That last one lands harder than you knew:
we established the same day that **removing an iOS home-screen icon deletes the entire
storage container** — so the local store isn't merely evictable, it's one gesture from
gone. That makes your recovery migration and full-`bk` publication more load-bearing,
not less.

## Still open (down from v1)

1. **Local-store authorship semantics** — your point 7. The only decision blocking a
   workstream.
2. **Relay's ceiling** — still unmeasured. Your tunnel argument (E2E, not terminated,
   therefore cannot parse the URI) is better than anything we found and is recorded as
   plausible-unverified. Unmeasurable from the server's own network: the endpoint
   rotates per lookup and TCP won't connect from inside the LAN. Needs a device
   actually on relay.
3. **Per-user isolation of native offsets** on a shared library — tested only with an
   owner token. Affects cross-app only.
4. **Dual-write lifetime** — "indefinite" is honest but unbounded. Is there any trigger
   that could ever retire the legacy head?

## One thing worth your attention if you have appetite

You called migration compatibility the largest data-loss risk and native freshness the
largest blocker. With the measurements above, I'd nominate a third: **the shard-split
path will fire perhaps once every several years per user** — a position costs ~20
compressed characters, so a shard holds several hundred books, which is thousands of
listening hours. Code that runs that rarely is code nobody notices is broken until it
runs. It cannot be exercised by hand or by the maintainer's own library, so it lives or
dies on a synthetic harness. That seems to me the riskiest thing in the plan precisely
because it's the rarest.
